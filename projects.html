PrimeDrive: The In-Transit Concierge

Stanford ME310 // Industry-Sponsored Capstone // Product Realization Team "Hotels" (ME310 cohort) — I led the mechanical design and hardware integration; teammates drove UX, software, and the staff/guest interfaces.

Luxury hotels promise a seamless, personal arrival, yet the experience often begins with a queue: check-in waits reach 20–50 minutes, and guest satisfaction drops by roughly 50% once a wait exceeds five minutes. Our team reframed the problem around the one calm, controlled moment that already exists before the lobby: the chauffeured car ride (offered by 85%+ of luxury properties). PrimeDrive is an in-vehicle concierge unit that completes check-in in transit, so the guest steps out of the car already checked in, keyed in, and known by name.

My Role — Mechanical Architecture

I owned the physical product: the concierge unit that lives in the rear cabin and carries every interactive subsystem. I developed the full enclosure in CAD and drove it from concept to a working, demoed prototype, integrating a touchscreen, a 4K camera for the live concierge video link, an ID/passport + QR scanner, an integrated payment terminal, and an NFC/RFID room-key module into one cohesive, premium housing.

  • Deployable display: designed an articulating, stow-and-present screen arm so the unit folds away discreetly and presents to a seated rear passenger at a comfortable viewing angle, packaged around real cabin and seat-back geometry.
  • Modular hardware bay: laid out a serviceable internal chassis that locates the camera, scanner, payment reader, and key module on a common datum, with routed cable management and ventilation for the compute and screen.
  • Key-issuance mechanism: integrated the NFC tap-to-program and physical key-card handoff into the fascia so the handover feels intentional and tamper-evident.
  • Mounting & DFM: designed a vehicle-agnostic mounting interface and detailed the housing for low-volume manufacture (draft, wall thickness, fastener bosses) using 3D-printed iterations.

Prototyping the Form Factor

Before converging on the seat-back console, I helped build and evaluate alternative delivery concepts — a mobile-robot unit and a drone hand-off — to pressure-test where and how to intercept the guest. Mapping the journey showed the car ride was the highest-value, lowest-friction intercept point, and the fixed in-cabin unit won on reliability, ergonomics, and trust.

Outcome

We delivered a functional prototype demonstrating the complete in-transit flow — video concierge, document scan, payment, and key issuance — and presented PrimeDrive to our industry partner. The concept extends naturally to shuttles and group pickups, cross-property partnerships, and airline/cruise transfers.

Vansh — The Lineage Watch Box

Stanford ME318 // 3-Axis CNC // Aluminum + ABS // Solo Project A solo design-and-build. "Vansh" (वंश) means lineage — the box carries a Suryavanshi (Sun-descendant) mandala honoring my Himachali Sood heritage.

Finished box: sandblasted 6061 base, sandblasted-ABS lid with machined Suryavanshi mandala, stainless dowel hinge, seating an Omega Seamaster Diver 300M.

I set out to machine a watch box worthy of my Omega Seamaster Diver 300M and the pieces I've built and collected — an object that doubles as a personal heirloom. After exploring organic directions (shells, gyroscopes, skull forms), I committed to a clean architectural box whose meaning lives in its lid: a Suryavanshi mandala machined in relief, a nod to lineage and clan.

6061 ALUMINUM BASE ABS LID SS DOWEL HINGE 0.5" FEM + BEM 3× CLOSE-SLIDING + 1× PRESS FIT

Design for Machinability

Working within 3-axis constraints, I simplified the geometry so nearly everything could be cut with a 0.5" flat and ball end mill, reserving tighter detail only for the hinge and lid interface. I designed the open/close experience around a stainless dowel hinge and a deliberate tolerance scheme — three close-sliding fits and one press-fit — and modeled the mandala so every feature was provably machinable.

CAM & Machining

  • Tuned toolpaths for cycle-time and verified all moves cleared 0.35" above the stock bottom; caught a potential spindle crash in simulation before it reached the machine.
  • Calculated feeds and speeds independently for aluminum and ABS (including custom ABS tooling), with dedicated drilling and reaming operations for the hinge bores.
  • The aluminum base came off the machine almost exactly as designed; I traced a minor internal-fillet mismatch to a true ball-mill diameter of 0.485" vs. the nominal 0.5" — a good lesson in tool runout vs. CAM assumptions.

Finishing — Turning a Defect into the Look

Sandblasting the aluminum gave a clean, premium, parting-line-free exterior. The ABS lid finished rougher, with visible adaptive-clearing steps — so I leaned in: test sandblasting produced a limestone/marble aesthetic, making the machining lines read as intentional texture rather than flaws.

Assembly & Honest Reflection

The stainless shaft transition-fit cleanly into the aluminum, but pressing it through the softer ABS fractured the material; I faced, filed, and re-drilled to recover, and the shaft now juts proudly from the sides for a robust, industrial look. The lid closes with a satisfying snap and opens easily. The one thing I'd change next: the lid won't hold itself open for display, so a detented or friction hinge is the clear next iteration.

CuRIOS: Mechanical Interfaces

Space Payload Engineering // 16U Bus // 12G Qualification Moving Universe Lab, UC Berkeley — payload mechanical lead.

I led the mechanical realization of mounting interfaces to integrate a Sony IMX455 CMOS detector into a standard 16U CubeSat bus. The challenge was sub-millimeter alignment stability relative to the telescope bore-sight through a 12G Falcon 9 launch environment, while thermally isolating the detector to minimize dark-current noise.

Engineering Strategy

I engineered a kinematic-style mount to decouple the optical bench from chassis deformation, using G10 fiberglass standoffs for thermal resistance and keeping the detector within −10°C to +40°C while the bus ran hotter.

Validation

  • GD&T (ASME Y14.5) tolerance analysis defining focal-plane flatness within 50 microns.
  • Thermal-vacuum cycling (−40°C to +80°C) and random-vibration qualification.
  • ~100× detector-cost reduction by qualifying COTS hardware for high-fidelity science.

Deployable Light-Shield Baffles

Space Mechanisms // Abaqus Nonlinear FEA // NASA GEVS

For time-domain astrophysics needing 21 Vega-magnitude sensitivity, stray-light rejection is a hard requirement. I designed a deployable light-shield that stows within 16U launch volume and extends reliably in orbit via a zero-backlash, spring-driven mechanism.

Nonlinear Analysis

I ran exhaustive Abaqus FEA with mesh-independence studies, refining tetrahedral elements below 0.1" until hinge-joint stress concentrations converged within 2.5%. The structure cleared 12G static-equivalent launch loads with a Factor of Safety > 1.5 against yield and buckling.

Qualification

Space-qualified via TVAC cycling at UC Berkeley, with MoS₂ coatings to prevent cold-welding in vacuum.

Multimaterial DIW Printhead

Microfluidics // Ansys Fluent CFD Team: Aryan Sood & design cohort.

Direct Ink Writing of soft matter is limited by cross-contamination during material switching. We designed a low-cost, high-throughput printhead that creates sharp voxels by minimizing residence time in the switching manifold.

Optimization & Performance

  • CFD: Ansys Fluent streamline analysis to locate and eliminate stagnant dead volumes.
  • Manufacturing: complex internal channels via high-resolution SLA (Rigid 10K resin).
  • Result: switching waste reduced 45%, with validated switching-quality metrics for viscoelastic inks.

Pneumatic Venus Flytrap

ME303 // Soft Robotics // Hyperelasticity Team: Simon Kliger, Aryan Sood, Manuel Abitia.

Inspired by the Venus flytrap's thigmonastic motion, we used pneumatic actuation for responsive motion in soft materials, moving from a teardrop design to a modular linear-chamber structure with a strain-limiting layer for predictable curvature.

Hyperelastic Simulation

Modeled in Abaqus with a Neo-Hookean material (shear modulus 27.6 kPa) and hybrid C3D8H elements for Ecoflex 00-30 incompressibility. Physical tests confirmed bending at 40.0 kPa within 5% of simulation.

Mechatronics

An Arduino Uno, L293D H-bridge, and ultrasonic sensing enable autonomous capture on object proximity.

Autonomous Ball Launcher

Mechatronics // PID Control // ESP32 Team: Aryan Sood, Fernando Medina, Masayuki Ohashi, Zeyad Alhomeida.

We built a tennis-ball launcher with an automatic feeder: dual-flywheel propulsion on independent 12V DC motors and a motorized 90° indexing bucket.

Mechatronic Architecture

A cascaded position controller on an ESP32 handles indexing; the PID loop holds flywheel RPM through ball contact with optical-encoder feedback, plus autonomous jam-detection via feeder-motor current monitoring.

Mechanical Fabrication

  • 3D-printed L-brackets and feeder buckets for lightweight structure.
  • 80/20 aluminum extrusion for a stiff, modular chassis.
  • Rubber flywheels with preloaded shaft transmissions for maximum energy transfer.

Predictive Wind Modeling

Instrumentation // Fluid Dynamics // ME103 Team: Aryan Sood, Julius Tereck, Matthew Kang, Yeiji Lee.

We calibrated a vertical-axis dual-blade anemometer in a wind tunnel to build a predictive wind-speed model across 5–22 m/s and varying angles of attack.

Modeling Results

Analytical drag models overestimated velocity due to unmodeled friction, while our linear regression performed excellently across the full range (R² > 0.98) — a clean demonstration that empirical modeling can beat first-principles approximations for low-cost mechanical sensors.

Algorithmic Guitar Stand

ME227 // Generative Design // Fusion 360

I used generative design to create an optimized structural support for electric guitars, minimizing mass while holding a Factor of Safety suitable for high-value instruments.

Generative Parameters

I defined a 40 lbf remote-force load case with directional placement bias and fed in custom Bambu PLA material data (2–6% elongation at break) so the solver respected real additive-manufacturing limits.

Outcome

The final design reached a functional FoS of 6–7 with 15% sparse infill; physical testing showed only 0.009" deflection under full load, validating the FEA.
