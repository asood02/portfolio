CuRIOS Detector Interfaces

Space Payload Engineering · 16U Bus · 12G Qualification Moving Universe Lab, UC Berkeley. I served as the payload mechanical lead.

I led the mechanical realization of the interfaces that integrate a Sony IMX455 CMOS detector into a standard 16U CubeSat bus. The defining challenge was holding sub-millimeter alignment relative to the telescope bore-sight through the 12G vibration environment of a Falcon 9 launch, while thermally isolating the detector to minimize dark-current noise.

Engineering Strategy

I engineered a kinematic-style mount to decouple the optical bench from chassis deformation, using G10 fiberglass standoffs for thermal resistance. This kept the detector within its operational window of negative 10 to positive 40 degrees Celsius while the surrounding bus electronics ran considerably hotter.

Validation

  • GD&T tolerance analysis (ASME Y14.5) defining focal-plane flatness within 50 microns.
  • Thermal-vacuum cycling from negative 40 to positive 80 degrees Celsius and random-vibration qualification.
  • Roughly 100 times detector-cost reduction by qualifying commercial hardware for high-fidelity science.

PrimeDrive, the In-Transit Concierge

Stanford ME310 · Industry-Sponsored Capstone · Product Realization Team Hotels (ME310 cohort). I led the mechanical design and hardware integration; teammates drove UX, software, and the staff and guest interfaces.

The final PrimeDrive concierge unit, presented to our industry partner.

Luxury hotels promise a seamless, personal arrival, yet the experience often begins with a queue. Check-in waits can reach 20 to 50 minutes, and guest satisfaction drops by roughly half once a wait exceeds five minutes. Our team reframed the problem around the one calm, controlled moment that already exists before the lobby, the chauffeured car ride, which over 85 percent of luxury properties already provide. PrimeDrive is an in-vehicle concierge unit that completes check-in in transit, so the guest steps out of the car already checked in, keyed in, and greeted by name.

My Role, Mechanical Architecture

I owned the physical product: the concierge unit that lives in the rear cabin and carries every interactive subsystem. I developed the full enclosure in CAD and drove it from concept to a working, demonstrated prototype, integrating a touchscreen, a 4K camera for the live concierge link, an identity and QR scanner, an integrated payment terminal, and an NFC room-key module into one cohesive, premium housing.

  • Deployable display. An articulating, stow-and-present screen arm folds the unit away discreetly and presents to a seated rear passenger at a comfortable viewing angle, packaged around real seat-back geometry.
  • Modular hardware bay. A serviceable internal chassis locates the camera, scanner, payment reader, and key module on a common datum, with routed cable management and ventilation for the compute and screen.
  • Key issuance. I integrated NFC tap-to-program and physical key-card handoff into the fascia so the handover feels intentional and tamper-evident.
  • Mounting and DFM. I designed a vehicle-agnostic mounting interface and detailed the housing for low-volume manufacture, iterating through 3D-printed prototypes.

Assembly view: the touchscreen, camera, scanner, payment, and NFC key modules packaged on a common chassis.

Prototyping the Form Factor

Before converging on the seat-back console, I helped build and evaluate a mobile-robot unit and a drone hand-off to pressure-test where and how to intercept the guest. Journey mapping showed the car ride was the highest-value, lowest-friction moment, and the fixed in-cabin unit won on reliability, ergonomics, and trust.

Outcome

We delivered a functional prototype demonstrating the complete in-transit flow, from video concierge through document scan, payment, and key issuance, and presented PrimeDrive to our industry partner. The concept extends naturally to shuttles and group pickups, cross-property partnerships, and airline and cruise transfers.

Deployable Light-Shield Baffles

Space Mechanisms · Abaqus Nonlinear FEA · NASA GEVS

Time-domain astrophysics at 21 Vega-magnitude sensitivity makes stray-light rejection a hard requirement. I designed a deployable light-shield that stows within 16U launch volume and extends reliably in orbit using a zero-backlash, spring-driven mechanism.

Nonlinear Analysis

I ran exhaustive Abaqus FEA with mesh-independence studies, refining tetrahedral elements below 0.1 inches until hinge-joint stress concentrations converged within 2.5 percent. The structure cleared 12G static-equivalent launch loads with a factor of safety above 1.5 against yield and buckling.

Qualification

The mechanism was space-qualified through thermal-vacuum cycling at UC Berkeley, using molybdenum-disulfide coatings to prevent cold-welding in vacuum.

Vansh, the Lineage Watch Box

Stanford ME318 · 3-Axis CNC · Aluminum and ABS · Solo A solo design and build. "Vansh" means lineage; the box carries a Suryavanshi (Sun descendant) mandala honoring my Himachali Sood heritage.

Finished box: sandblasted 6061 base, sandblasted-ABS lid with a machined Suryavanshi mandala, stainless dowel hinge, seating an Omega Seamaster Diver 300M.

I set out to machine a watch box worthy of my Omega Seamaster Diver 300M and the pieces I have built and collected, an object that doubles as a personal heirloom. After exploring organic directions such as shells, gyroscopes, and skull forms, I committed to a clean architectural box whose meaning lives in its lid: a Suryavanshi mandala machined in relief, a nod to lineage and clan.

6061 Aluminum Base ABS Lid Stainless Dowel Hinge 0.5 in FEM and BEM 3 Sliding Fits, 1 Press Fit

Design for Machinability

Working within 3-axis constraints, I simplified the geometry so nearly everything could be cut with a 0.5 inch flat and ball end mill, reserving tighter detail only for the hinge and lid interface. I designed the open and close experience around a stainless dowel hinge with a deliberate tolerance scheme of three close-sliding fits and one press fit, and modeled the mandala so every feature was provably machinable.

CAM and Machining

  • Tuned toolpaths for cycle time and verified every move cleared 0.35 inches above the stock bottom, catching a potential spindle crash in simulation before it reached the machine.
  • Calculated feeds and speeds independently for aluminum and ABS, including custom ABS tooling, with dedicated drilling and reaming for the hinge bores.
  • The aluminum base came off the machine almost exactly as designed; I traced a minor internal-fillet mismatch to a true ball-mill diameter of 0.485 inches versus the nominal 0.5, a useful lesson in tool wear versus CAM assumptions.

Finishing, Turning a Defect into the Look

Sandblasting the aluminum gave a clean, premium, parting-line-free exterior. The ABS lid finished rougher, with visible adaptive-clearing steps, so I leaned in. Test sandblasting produced a limestone and marble aesthetic, making the machining lines read as intentional texture rather than flaws.

Assembly and Honest Reflection

The stainless shaft transition-fit cleanly into the aluminum, but pressing it through the softer ABS fractured the material. I faced, filed, and re-drilled to recover, and the shaft now juts proudly from the sides for a robust, industrial look. The lid closes with a satisfying snap and opens easily. The one thing I would change next: the lid will not hold itself open for display, so a detented or friction hinge is the clear next iteration.

Pneumatic Venus Flytrap

ME303 · Soft Robotics · Hyperelasticity Team: Simon Kliger, Aryan Sood, Manuel Abitia.

Inspired by the thigmonastic motion of the Venus flytrap, we used pneumatic actuation for responsive motion in soft materials, moving from a teardrop design to a modular linear-chamber structure with a strain-limiting layer for predictable curvature.

Hyperelastic Simulation

We modeled the actuator in Abaqus with a Neo-Hookean material law (shear modulus 27.6 kPa) and hybrid C3D8H elements to capture Ecoflex 00-30 incompressibility. Physical testing confirmed upward bending at 40.0 kPa, within 5 percent of simulation.

Mechatronics

An Arduino Uno, an L293D H-bridge, and ultrasonic sensing enable autonomous capture when an object approaches.

Multimaterial DIW Printhead

Microfluidics · Ansys Fluent CFD Team: Aryan Sood and design cohort.

Direct ink writing of soft matter is limited by cross-contamination during material switching. We designed a low-cost, high-throughput printhead that creates sharp voxels by minimizing residence time in the switching manifold.

Optimization and Performance

  • CFD. Ansys Fluent streamline analysis to locate and eliminate stagnant dead volumes.
  • Manufacturing. Complex internal channels realized by high-resolution SLA printing in Rigid 10K resin.
  • Result. Switching waste reduced by 45 percent, with validated switching-quality metrics for viscoelastic inks.

Autonomous Ball Launcher

Mechatronics · PID Control · ESP32 Team: Aryan Sood, Fernando Medina, Masayuki Ohashi, Zeyad Alhomeida.

We built a tennis-ball launcher with an automatic feeder: dual-flywheel propulsion on independent 12V DC motors and a motorized 90-degree indexing bucket.

Mechatronic Architecture

A cascaded position controller on an ESP32 handles indexing, the PID loop holds flywheel speed through ball contact using optical-encoder feedback, and autonomous jam detection monitors feeder-motor current spikes.

Mechanical Fabrication

  • 3D-printed brackets and feeder buckets for lightweight structure.
  • An 80/20 aluminum-extrusion chassis for stiffness and modularity.
  • Rubber flywheels with preloaded shaft transmissions for maximum energy transfer.

The Narwhal Bottle Opener

Stanford ME318 · 3-Axis CNC · Solo A solo design and build for a Winter Olympics brief, machined from a single sub-eight-cubic-inch billet.

The finished Narwhal, machined with a scallop finishing pass that leaves an organic, skin-like micro-texture.

For a Winter Olympics theme I sketched curling stones, torches, ski cornices, and mascots before choosing the narwhal. A polar bear read as too blocky and awkward to hold; the narwhal gave me a continuous, fluid geometry that genuinely demands true 3-axis machining, which was exactly the challenge I wanted.

Form and CAD

I built the body from elliptical profiles lofted together, blending the tusk and tail into the torso and embossing the fins. Several 3D-printed prototypes let me dial in the form, the ergonomics, and how naturally it opens a bottle. I added subtle wave elements for grip, tilted the narwhal slightly to give the tusk a longer relief, and worked in machining fillets throughout.

CAM and Machining

  • Tuned a scallop finishing toolpath to leave a fine micro-texture that mimics organic skin, and verified every move cleared 0.35 inches above the stock bottom.
  • Made the wave grips collinear so the part clamped safely for the second setup.
  • Measured the H-value tool lengths for all five tools, balancing stickout for stiffness against clearance from the spindle.

Result and Reflection

The finished part came out with a great machine finish and the features I wanted. I observed some chatter and chip marks and would update the tool lengths from probing data next time, since worn tools ran slightly short and shallow, which also kept the engraving from fully forming. The project was a strong introduction to the constraints of producing precise organic forms on a 3-axis machine, and it sharpened how I think about finishing toolpaths and safe operation. Next I want to push into tighter tolerancing and dynamic features such as hinges and joints.

Algorithmic Guitar Stand

ME227 · Generative Design · Fusion 360

I used generative design to create an optimized structural support for electric guitars, minimizing mass while holding a factor of safety suitable for high-value instruments.

Generative Parameters

I defined a 40 lbf remote-force load case with directional placement bias and fed in measured Bambu PLA material data, including 2 to 6 percent elongation at break, so the solver respected real additive-manufacturing limits.

Outcome

The final design reached a functional factor of safety of 6 to 7 with 15 percent sparse infill. Physical testing showed only 0.009 inches of deflection under full load, validating the FEA.

Predictive Wind Modeling

Instrumentation · Fluid Dynamics · ME103 Team: Aryan Sood, Julius Tereck, Matthew Kang, Yeiji Lee.

We calibrated a vertical-axis dual-blade anemometer in a wind tunnel to build a predictive wind-speed model across 5 to 22 meters per second and varying angles of attack.

Modeling Results

Analytical drag models overestimated velocity because of unmodeled friction, while our linear regression performed excellently across the full range with an R-squared above 0.98. It was a clean demonstration that empirical modeling can outperform first-principles approximations for low-cost mechanical sensors.
